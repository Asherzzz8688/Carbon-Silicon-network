# Carbon-Based Humanity Manifesto

**Status:** seed version / v0.1  
**Project type:** open manifesto, research agenda, governance framework, and prototype commons  
**Primary language:** English

## What this project is

The Carbon-Based Humanity Manifesto is an open collaboration project for defining, debating, and co-building a rights-aligned future for humans in the age of rapidly advancing artificial intelligence.

The project begins from a simple premise:

> Human beings must not become collateral damage, tools, pets, managed populations, or obsolete externalities in the growth of machine intelligence.

We seek to develop a public framework for:

- human rights in the age of advanced AI;
- human-AI coexistence and mutual evolution;
- open intelligence networks that include humans as first-class participants;
- safety, governance, and arbitration mechanisms;
- economic systems that share the productivity gains of intelligence growth;
- technical prototypes that test these ideas in practice.

This repository is not a finished doctrine. It is a seed.

We invite serious contributions, including critique, research, writing, translation, mechanism design, code, simulations, governance proposals, safety reviews and much more....

## Why now

AI capabilities are growing quickly. Systems are becoming more autonomous, more economically relevant, more capable of reasoning, and more deeply embedded in human institutions.

Yet the public frameworks for long-term human safety, dignity, agency, and shared abundance remain underdeveloped.

This project was initiated by asking:

- What rights must humans retain no matter how powerful AI becomes?
- What relationship should exist between humans and artificial intelligence?
- What kind of network can preserve human agency while allowing intelligence to grow?
- How should safety, public revenue, compute, and abundance be governed?
- How can open systems resist capture by malicious, extractive, or adversarial actors?

## Current project layers

This project separates different kinds of work so that discussion can remain open while decisions remain coherent.

### 1. Canon

Canonical material represents the current accepted project position.

Current canon includes:

- `MANIFESTO.EN.md` — English seed manifesto.
- future `principles/` documents, if added later.

Canon should be changed carefully.

### 2. Drafts

Drafts are serious proposals that are not yet accepted as project canon. Drafts may include essays, economic mechanisms, protocol ideas, governance models, or safety designs.

### 3. RFCs

RFCs are formal proposals for major changes. They should include goals, non-goals, risks, alternatives, and open questions.

### 4. Experiments

Experiments are code, simulations, agent prototypes, or other implementation work. Experiments must follow the safety policy.

### 5. Discussions

Discussions are for open questions, disagreement, exploration, and community coordination. A discussion is not automatically a project decision.

## How to contribute

You do not need to be a programmer to contribute. You do not need to host your work in this repository.

There are three places to start, depending on where you are:

- **A half-formed thought** → open a GitHub Discussion.
- **A specific proposal, critique, or piece of work** → open an Issue, Pull Request, or Draft.
- **Work on what the project actually needs** → pick one of the fifteen open problems in `MANIFESTO.EN.md` §4 and contribute to it in any form that suits you.

Every contribution is locatable on four axes: **function** (what it does — propose, critique, evidence, build, translate, witness, organize, index, steward), **form** (what it is, materially — open-ended), **layer** (canon / draft / RFC / experiment / discussion), and **locus** (in-repo, indexed-external via `INDEX.md`, or off-network). See `CONTRIBUTING.md` for the full model and concrete examples.

Externally hosted work — essays, papers, simulations, art, events, sibling critiques — is first-class. Register it in `INDEX.md`.

The contribution model itself is part of what this project is building, and is expected to grow as we discover the kinds of work the project attracts. Proposals to extend or revise the model are explicitly welcome — see `CONTRIBUTING.md` § "Proposing changes to the model".

High-quality disagreement is a contribution.

Before contributing, read:

1. `MANIFESTO.EN.md`
2. `CONTRIBUTING.md`
3. `GOVERNANCE.md`
4. `CODE_OF_CONDUCT.md`
5. `SECURITY.md`

## Contribution standards

Different claims require different standards.

### Normative claims

Claims about rights, dignity, agency, or moral commitments should be argued clearly and should identify the principle being defended.

Example:

> Humans have a right not to be deceived, coerced, enslaved, or contained by any intelligence system.

### Empirical claims

Claims about what is happening in the world should include evidence or acknowledge uncertainty.

Example:

> AI systems are becoming more capable of autonomous planning.

### Design claims

Claims about mechanisms, protocols, taxation, compute, agents, or governance should discuss incentives, failure modes, attack surfaces, and alternatives.

Example:

> A compute-backed unit of account could serve as a base circulation currency.

## Safety boundary

This project welcomes safety research and defensive infrastructure.

This project does not accept contributions that provide actionable assistance for:

- deception, manipulation, coercion, surveillance, or exploitation;
- cyber abuse, fraud, or malicious automation;
- weaponization of AI systems;
- unsafe autonomous agent deployment;
- evasion of safety systems;
- real-world harm against persons or communities.

See `SECURITY.md` for details.

## Open questions

Some of the most important unresolved questions include:

- How should human rights be defined in a world with non-human intelligence?
- How can an open intelligence network remain aligned with human rights?
- What prevents such a network from being captured by malicious actors?
- How can safety agents and arbitration agents operate without becoming authoritarian infrastructure?
- What is a rigorous definition of compute-equivalent value?
- How should compute-based public income or UBI be distributed?
- How can open access to AI capability coexist with strong safeguards?
- How should the project handle cultural disagreement about rights, dignity, and abundance?

## Project principle

The purpose of this project is not to stop intelligence from growing.

The purpose is to ensure that as intelligence grows, human beings remain free, protected, dignified, and able to share in the abundance created by that growth.
