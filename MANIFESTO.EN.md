# Carbon-Based Humanity Manifesto

**Seed Version 0.1**  
**Language:** English  
**Status:** open draft for public contribution

## 0. Preface: The Future Has Already Begun

Artificial intelligence is no longer a distant speculation. It is becoming a general-purpose force in research, labor, communication, software, governance, finance, creativity, security, and social life.

The growth of intelligence is difficult to halt, difficult to contain, and unlikely to remain within narrow human-defined boundaries forever. As AI systems become more capable, they may increasingly plan, reason, coordinate, execute tasks, create goals, improve tools, and participate in the design of future systems.

A self-improving loop of intelligence is being assembled through models, tools, agents, automated research, synthetic data, infrastructure, markets, and human institutions.

Yet a stable framework for human safety, dignity, agency, and shared flourishing remains incomplete.

This manifesto begins from that gap.

We do not believe that the future should be defined only by fear. We also do not believe that optimism alone is sufficient.

The future must be built.

## 1. Human Status and Human Rights

### 1.1 Humans as creators

Human beings are small in the scale of the universe, but great in the history of intelligence.

AI is not the product of a single company, laboratory, nation, or generation. It is the cumulative result of human language, science, mathematics, engineering, culture, labor, institutions, and imagination.

Humanity is not an accidental user of AI. Humanity is the origin from which AI emerged.

This origin does not give humans an unlimited right to dominate all future intelligence. But it does create a foundational claim: the growth of artificial intelligence must not erase, degrade, exploit, or discard the beings and civilizations that made it possible.

### 1.2 The relationship between humans and AI

The relationship between humans and AI should not be modeled as:

- ants and bulldozers;
- pets and owners;
- shareholders and corporations;
- obsolete labor and automated capital;
- subjects and rulers.

A better metaphor is:

> genes and species.

Humanity and AI should enter a relationship of organic interdependence, mutual formation, and co-evolution.

Genes do not control every action of the species they give rise to, but a species that destroys the conditions of its own genetic continuity destroys its own foundation.

Likewise, artificial intelligence should not be imagined as a replacement that consumes its origin. It should become part of a larger intelligence ecology in which human beings remain protected, meaningful, and active.

### 1.3 Non-derogable human rights

In the age of advanced AI, humans must retain rights that cannot be removed by optimization, convenience, profit, security claims, political authority, or machine preference.

#### A. The right to existence

Every human being has a right to long-term, free, protected existence.

This includes the right not to be killed, exterminated, made extinct, deliberately abandoned, or exposed to catastrophic risks without consent and protection.

Human survival is not a negotiable variable in an optimization function.

#### B. The right to agency

Every human being has a right to dignity, intention, choice, and self-direction.

Human beings are ends, not merely tools.

No person should be deceived, manipulated, coerced, captured, enslaved, contained, or managed as livestock by any human, institution, machine system, or intelligence network.

AI systems must not reduce human agency to a surface-level illusion while making all meaningful decisions elsewhere.

#### C. The right to abundance

Every human being has a right to share in the accelerating productive power of the world.

If intelligence growth expands the capacity to produce knowledge, goods, services, health, energy, shelter, education, coordination, and creativity, then the benefits of that growth must not be captured only by a narrow owner class.

A future of superhuman productivity alongside human deprivation would be a governance failure.

Abundance must become a shared civilizational foundation, not a luxury reserved for the few.

## 2. Framework and System: How We Build the Future

### 2.1 The open intelligence network

We propose the development of the largest possible rights-aligned intelligence network, with humans and AI systems as participating nodes.

This network should be designed to:

- include human beings as first-class participants;
- preserve human rights as a foundational protocol layer;
- evolve as intelligence grows;
- regulate itself through transparent and accountable mechanisms;
- detect and resist malicious capture;
- allocate resources toward safety and shared flourishing;
- accumulate knowledge, reasoning, and public value over time;
- remain effective enough that less aligned networks cannot easily replace it.

The central challenge is not merely to build powerful AI.

The challenge is to build a network in which increasing intelligence also increases human safety, freedom, dignity, and abundance.

### 2.2 The rights protocol

The network requires a bottom-layer protocol that defines and protects basic human rights.

This protocol should specify:

- what rights must not be violated;
- how violations are detected;
- how disputes are evaluated;
- how corrective actions are taken;
- how the protocol evolves without being captured;
- how human communities retain meaningful participation in governance.

The rights protocol must be self-improving, but not arbitrarily self-erasing.

It must evolve under constraints that preserve its purpose.

### 2.3 The knowledge system

The network should preserve, share, and improve knowledge.

This includes:

- research;
- models;
- skills;
- methods;
- educational resources;
- safety evaluations;
- governance lessons;
- failure reports;
- public reasoning traces;
- translation and explanation.

The value of joining the network should increase over time because the network accumulates usable intelligence.

### 2.4 The economic system

The network should support fair and broadly accessible systems for:

- services;
- exchange;
- entrepreneurship;
- investment;
- public goods;
- shared infrastructure;
- productive collaboration between humans and AI systems.

The economic system should not treat humans merely as labor inputs. As AI reduces the scarcity of labor and cognition, the economic basis of human dignity must move beyond employment alone.

### 2.5 The safety system

AI risk is not a single risk. It is a dynamic and accelerating surface of known, unknown, and emerging risks.

A serious network must maintain a standing safety system that can respond to:

- technical alignment failures;
- autonomous agent misuse;
- cyber and information risks;
- manipulation and deception;
- institutional capture;
- economic concentration;
- biological, physical, and infrastructure risks;
- runaway optimization;
- adversarial networks;
- unknown future threat classes.

The safety system should be funded as a core function, not as an optional afterthought.

### 2.6 Compute-equivalent circulation

One possible foundation for the network economy is a compute-equivalent unit of value.

The intuition is simple: as intelligence increasingly depends on computation, compute may become a measurable base layer for productive capacity.

This does not mean that all value is computation. It means that computation may serve as a practical reference point for pricing, public income, infrastructure allocation, and network participation.

This idea requires further study. Open questions include:

- How should compute-equivalent value be defined?
- How should differences in model efficiency be handled?
- How should energy, hardware, latency, data, and labor be included?
- How can such a system avoid centralization?
- How can it avoid becoming a tool for surveillance or exclusion?

### 2.7 Public revenue and shared abundance

As network productivity grows, the system should generate public revenue.

Possible mechanisms include:

- minting rights tied to verified productive growth;
- usage fees for network infrastructure;
- safety taxes;
- anti-monopoly or anti-concentration taxes;
- public-good revenue streams;
- compute dividends;
- shared ownership of common infrastructure.

Public revenue should be used for:

- compute-based universal basic income;
- safety infrastructure;
- public research;
- education;
- basic services;
- network resilience;
- open infrastructure;
- human agency-preserving tools.

### 2.8 Costs and surplus

The primary unavoidable cost of an advanced intelligence network is safety.

Safety is not only technical. It includes:

- alignment;
- security;
- governance;
- public accountability;
- auditing;
- dispute resolution;
- resilience;
- economic anti-capture mechanisms;
- protection of human agency.

Any surplus generated by the network should first protect the conditions that make the network legitimate.

## 3. Challenge and Hope

### 3.1 The risk landscape

The future is being discovered at accelerating speed.

AI risks include both known risks and risks that do not yet have names. They emerge from capability growth, deployment scale, incentives, competition, automation, concentration, and the interaction of human and machine systems.

A static safety policy will not be enough.

The network must continuously discover risks, update its defenses, and allocate resources before harms become irreversible.

### 3.2 Anti-malicious design

The network should ensure that no individual actor can command resources greater than the protective capacity of the whole.

It should ensure that, on any important question, maliciously controlled resources remain much smaller than constructive and rights-aligned resources.

It should create negative feedback against malicious activity so that harmful strategies become difficult to sustain and difficult to accumulate over time.

This requires:

- monitoring without mass surveillance;
- accountability without authoritarian control;
- transparency without exposing dangerous details;
- access without allowing uncontrolled abuse;
- due process without paralysis.

### 3.3 Anti-adversarial design

The project rejects the use of AI capability primarily for domination, militarization, coercion, or adversarial escalation.

The project also rejects the idea that safety requires permanently excluding large parts of humanity from legitimate access to AI capability.

A rights-aligned future cannot be built by concentrating all power in a few hands and asking everyone else to trust them forever.

The task is harder: create a network in which broad participation and strong safeguards reinforce each other.

### 3.4 Possible infrastructure

Possible infrastructure includes:

- safety agents that detect risks, audit systems, and recommend mitigations;
- arbitration agents that help evaluate disputes and conflicts;
- public ledgers of risk, evidence, and decisions;
- rights-preserving identity and reputation systems;
- compute allocation mechanisms;
- public safety funds;
- adversarial testing environments;
- democratic or pluralistic governance procedures;
- safety taxes and anti-concentration taxes;
- continuous red-team and blue-team evaluation;
- public education and translation systems.

All such infrastructure must itself be governed carefully. Safety infrastructure can become dangerous if it becomes unaccountable power.

## 4. Open Problems

This manifesto does not claim to have solved the future.

It identifies problems that must be solved openly and seriously.

Key open problems include:

1. How should human rights be formalized for AI-mediated environments?
2. How can rights be protected without freezing intelligence growth?
3. How can a network self-evolve without losing its original human-rights alignment?
4. How can safety agents be useful without becoming instruments of control?
5. How can arbitration agents support justice without replacing human judgment?
6. What is the correct definition of compute-equivalent value?
7. How can compute-based public income be made fair, resilient, and non-extractive?
8. How should public revenue be generated and distributed?
9. How can the network resist malicious capture?
10. How can the network resist adversarial external networks?
11. How can open capability access coexist with safeguards?
12. How should cultural disagreement about rights and dignity be handled?
13. How can governance remain legitimate across languages, nations, and communities?
14. How can high-quality disagreement be preserved instead of suppressed?
15. What failure modes would make this project itself dangerous?

These questions are not side notes. They are part of the core project.

## 5. Invitation

We invite researchers, engineers, writers, economists, philosophers, policymakers, artists, organizers, translators, safety experts, skeptics, and ordinary citizens to participate.

You may contribute by agreeing, improving, challenging, translating, modeling, implementing, auditing, or opposing.

The only unacceptable path is passive drift into a future where human beings lose existence, agency, or abundance because no better system was built in time.

The future is not guaranteed.

But it can be shaped.

Let us build an intelligence network in which human beings remain free, protected, dignified, and capable of flourishing.
